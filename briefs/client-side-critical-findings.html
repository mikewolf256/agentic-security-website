Executive brief: handling client-side “critical” findings

Audience: CTO / VP Engineering / Security Leadership • Reading time: ~3 minutes

The problem

Your scanner just flagged a pile of “Critical” vulnerabilities. Your team is panicking and your next leadership update is looming.

Before you mobilize engineering: scanner severity ≠ exploitability. Many client-side “critical” findings are false positives.

The decision framework

Step 1: categorize the finding

Category Examples Typical FP rate
Client-side injection XSS, prototype pollution, DOM-based attacks 40–60%
Auth/session issues JWT problems, session fixation 50–70%
Configuration Missing headers, CORS, CSP 30–50%
Business logic IDOR, access control 20–40%

Rule of thumb: the more the scanner relies on response heuristics (vs. proof), the higher the false positive rate.

Step 2: ask three questions

  • Can we reproduce it? A human can demonstrate the attack, not just a scanner report.
  • What’s the actual impact? “XSS in admin” is not the same as “XSS on a dead 404.”
  • What’s the exploit path? Auth required? Social engineering? Rare conditions?

Step 3: validate before remediating

Don’t allocate sprint capacity based on scanner severity alone. Do validate critical findings first and attach evidence to the decision.

Real-world example

Scanner output: 4 Critical NoSQL/LDAP “auth bypass” candidates (CVSS 9.1)

After validation: 0 confirmed. Endpoint returned homepage for any request; scanner confused “200 OK” with “success.”

Engineering hours saved: 40+

When to escalate immediately

  • RCE/command injection with PoC
  • SQL injection with data extraction proof
  • Auth bypass on real production endpoints
  • Exposed secrets/credentials
  • Active exploitation indicators

Key differentiator: these have proof, not just alerts.
